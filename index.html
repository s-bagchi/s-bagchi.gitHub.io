London: Creating the City as Home

Focusing on the environment of London, in its physical, infrastructural, sensorial and digital form, its perception can be incredibly subjective and unique, depending on the eyes of the beholder. In particular, examining London as a 'new' environment yields insights into how people adapt to new environments.

In this research project, I look and document the experiences of 4 international students studying at UCL, assigned letters for the purpose of anonymity. I highlight their experiences of making London their home as people who have moved here recently from different countries. When asked about what makes a place home, several themes arise: being familiar with and knowledgeable about a place, having material comforts, having a sense of community or people who are close to you. However, these are relatively abstract. When asked about their actual day-to-day experiences of becoming more familiar with London, what culture shocks they were dealing with, conversations mainly focused on food. Having come from places like India and Hong Kong, they missed home food, and a significant part of their time (without being conscious of it) was dedicated to finding that sense of home and comfort through food. Their interactions with London, be it through the places they visited the most, or through the apps on their phone, highlighted this as important.
While my initial focus was on this aspect, utilising a more holistic perspective, I realised the connection between food, kinship and place for my participants. This relationship between the three has been recorded in literature, connected to more traditional anthropological themes around creating home and the role of kinship and sustenance (Carsten, 1997); however, I highlight a blurring of boundaries between home and city, and underline the role of the city and its physical and digital environment (Blunt & Sheringham, 2019). The way my participants chose where to live in London and the experience of finding this place relied on digital networks and technologies, which also informed their experiences with food, be it trying to live near an Asian grocery store or communal cooking practices. Here, I argue that digital technologies, be it more everyday technologies like Google Maps, communication over Facebook and WhatsApp groups, or delivery apps, can shape how one gets to know a city in the absence of prior knowledge. It helps you become more familiar with a city, find ways to make life more comfortable, and allows people to have more connections to have a sense of community— therefore, they create a sense of home.

Participants A, B, and D are from India and live in a university hall, and Participant C is from Hong Kong and lives in North London with flatmates. I conducted in-person and online interviews, hanging out in communal spaces like common rooms and kitchens and outside, app studies and social mapping. As a point of departure, I use screenshots and social mapping to give a broad overview of their physical and digital environments. After the following fieldwork, using pictures and screenshots, I highlight a chronological journey for my participants, from finding a place to live, to realising how food is important, to navigating London, and finally documenting their progress for their family.

Smartphone as Digital Home: "London Apps"


Looking at my participants' phones revealed how their phones have changed in terms of the apps they have. For example, after having moved to London, their phones have apps either specific to London or only downloaded because they moved here (e.g. Citymapper, TfL, Deliveroo, etc.). These “London Apps” often exist next to apps specific to other cities they lived in (Bangalore, Hong Kong, etc.), underlining how apps in general create relationships to your space or city, as well as visually highlighting how their digital environments are affected by their physical environments. More often than not, to make space for new ones, the “Home City Apps” get deleted. These apps are also very much part of living in London, and necessary day-to-day.
The primary apps in common are related to travel, food delivery apps, and supermarkets and cafés as well as apps related to money and banking. In the pictures, the underlined apps are London-specific apps, and the circled ones are from their previous cities.

Mapping the City


I asked my participants to create a map of their 'worlds' in London, as a way of illustrating their relationships with the city and to show their familiarity with places close to them: for example, places they visit regularly like university or grocery stores, and other places they feel is important to them. Beyond home and work, the important places they consider important are usually to do with food: general grocery stores, Asian supermarkets, cafés, restaurants, kebab shops, etc. These are the aspects of the city salient to them on a map, as places they notice as part of their life, or places they travel to. This way of centering home and food is the primary focus of my research.

Finding a Place to Live and the Role of Community

The London rental market can be difficult, especially for someone who is navigating it solely through digital means. For most people, this was the first channel to finding out what living in London would be like, interacting with future landlords, flatmate or brokers. Participant C described the experience of writing a message to someone on Spareroom as similar to 'writing a cover letter'.
“I looked on Spareroom, Zoopla and Rightmove, and it was so stressful. They give you the impression that there are endless options and you can always find a place. But then you feel the competition when you start messaging and there is no response. I had to customise my response, explain why I wanted to live there.”
Looking for accommodation also involved finding networks of people in London to rely on and exchange notes. For example, Participant A and Participant B met each other on a university WhatsApp group for international students. They both chose to live in student accommodations because they would be able to meet more people and have a better sense of community. This started on the WhatsApp groups, where people used these places to compare notes and rant about the university or accommodation.
Participant C also found her future flatmate on a UCL postgrad Facebook group, and reached out to her to start flat-hunting together. Before she moved to London, she recalls speaking to a friend, asking for advice on where to live, which first informed her understanding of London. She chose to live in Finsbury Park, looking up the area on Google Maps. “I liked that there was this whole street of cafés and restaurants […] I saw the cute coffee shops when I came here, and imagined bringing my laptop to work in them”.

If food be the food of love

“Home can be a lot of things […] for me, it is this dish my mom used to make in the Sunday mornings” — Participant A
Food was an especially important part of feeling a sense of home in a new city. Every participant talks about wanting to eat food which is consistent with what they had at home. Participant D describes her initial experiences of grocery shopping as paying very close attention to prices, and it taking a toll on her wellbeing. She responds to these pressures around balancing expenses while also eating tasty and healthy food by creating weekly meal plans and preps, saying that “it was better for [her] mental health if [she] didn't have to think too much about what to eat”. However, having good food was an important part of creating a sense of home. Thus, we see how these people manage to have this experience with the resources they have.
Participant B is a keen cook, and misses home food in particular. After moving to London, as a way to have nice food without ordering too much, his cooking “has become much fancier here than at home”, saying that whenever he sees the price of delivery, he “realises he can just make it at home”. On a similar vein, Participant C says that Chinese food is too expensive in London, which is why she finds it cheaper and easier to cook meals like pasta or salads with Western ingredients. Still, comfort food is incredibly important for her, saying that she “always has dumplings in the fridge”.

Haves and Have Nots in London

As a global city, London has pockets of East and South Asian grocery stores all over. For example, for Participants A, B, and C, the closest Indian grocery store to them was on Drummond Street, a place people were told about on the WhatsApp group for Indian students at UCL. The closeness of these stores to home is cited as a comfort factor, with Participant D saying that one of the best things about where she lived was having multiple Asian grocery stores nearby.
Creating a sense of home is not just about holding on to practices from the past, but also adapting and taking on new characteristics. For example, Participant A does not cook, but instead he likes to explore the options of different restaurants on delivery apps, buying meals so that he eats once a day (a habit from medical school). He finds this to be a major attraction in London, that you can access global cuisines, and he enjoys experimenting with food. Besides food delivery apps, he also likes going to the TianTian supermarket, and really likes makgeoli (Korean rice wine). He says, “I had never had it in India before, and ever since I found it, I keep some in the fridge”.
London is not only defined by what it has, but also what it doesn't. For example, Participant B talks about how the chili powder in London doesn't taste right or as good as it should, and “made for British people”, which is why he has brought supplies like spices from home. Similarly, Participant C talks about buying a rice cooker on a Chinese site called hktv, and paying to get it delivered in London, citing that “the taste of rice is different”. It is clearly seen that people make considerable effort to replicate and hold on to experiences of food which are culturally specific (like being able to taste the specific aspects of rice or chili powder). They can also make special preparations to manage time, for instance, with Participant D bringing a pressure cooker from home to make quick and tasty meals, and freezing chopped onion, garlic, and masala mixes (cumin, coriander, chili powder, and turmeric) to add to her food.

Food and Kinship

Cooking food also has significant connections to maintaining relationships and spending time with friends nearby and family abroad. For example, Participant A spends a lot of time in his friends' kitchens at his university hall, with cooking not only becoming a communal bonding activity, but also “a way to have nice food”. On the other end, Participant B invites people over when he is cooking, He also highlights how he only walks to grocery stores when he is with friends. Additionally, an important part of cooking is documenting it through taking pictures, and sending it to family. This process illuminates several things: showing their pride over cooking something good, to entertain, inform or ask for tips, and even to demonstrate that they are taking care of themselves
